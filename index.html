About

At UBC MIST, our team strives to augment the UBC biomedical engineering student community's capacity to participate in, and contribute to, the larger conversation on healthcare as well as the biomedical technology in it. Our central approach to achieving this is through participation in biomedical engineering competitions around the world, where we put forward new solutions to pressing medical challenges and help build the technical abilities of our team members.

2020/2021

Many of us are conscious of our daily stresses and anxiety, but struggle to understand and track our emotional state over the long term. Journaling is useful when seeking professional help, but it can be difficult to monitor one's state effectively from one day to the next.

This year, following the need for remote work and an increase in people looking to start the journey towards managing their stress, UBC MIST has chosen to revisit our previous Project Beacon.

With a new focus on managing self-reported data, a more reliable machine learning system, and an emphasis on understanding how stress is measured and reported, our team is hoping to make a targeted impact and provide a tool to help users build a unique profile for when they chose to seek professional advice.

2018/2019

2018 was the founding year of UBC MIST, a passion project driven by a group of dedicated students.

With our team's model being driven by a new innovation every year, MIST centered the first project around mental health.While researching projects for the team, we realized that mental health is a key topic in medicine but is not given as much attention in engineering. This was the catalyst that sparked our interest in the topic, as we believe that there is a lot of room for innovation and growth in this particular field.

Our app, Beacon, is connected to a wearable device which tracks the userâ€™s biometric data. Our machine learning technology creates a unique user profile which allows the user to gain insight into their stress levels throughout daily life.

2019/2020

Our team was fortunate to collaborate with the Praxis Spinal Cord Institute (previously known as the Rick Hansen Institute), giving us insight into the importance of power assist devices for manual wheelchairs. Manual propelling a wheelchair for long stretches of time can lead to shoulder injuries, and many areas have terrain unsuitable for wheelchairs. Additionally, some patients with spinal cord injuries suffer impaired hand functions, thus cannot properly propel wheelchairs forward. However, these devices can cost upwards of $10,000 and can be a necessary but unreachable technology for many. As a result, our team looked to create an affordable wheelchair power assist device for developing countries.

Unfortunately, due to the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic, our project had to be halted in favour of prioritizing the safety of our team. This project may be continued in the future.
